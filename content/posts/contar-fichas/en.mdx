The first time my grandfather beat me without ever looking me in the eyes, I understood that domino was not a luck game. He had the tiles counted. He knew exactly what I could have — and he played accordingly.

Counting tiles isn't cheating. It's the difference between playing domino and *understanding* domino.

### What Does "Counting Tiles" Mean?

In Venezuelan pair domino, the game starts with 28 tiles distributed among 4 players — 7 each. The remaining 4 go to the boneyard (except in certain variants with no boneyard). This means at any moment, information is available: what you see in your hand, what's already on the board, and what's missing.

Counting tiles is the process of mentally tracking which numbers have come out, which haven't, and deducing — with probabilities — what your partner might hold and what your opponents might hold.

### The Math Behind the Double

Every number from 0 to 6 appears in exactly 7 tiles. If four tiles with the number 5 have already hit the board, only three tiles with that number remain in the players' hands. If you hold one of those three, the other two are split among the remaining players.

Did your partner pass when the 5 was open? You just eliminated them as the holder of those two tiles. The opponents have them. That changes everything.

You don't need to be a mathematician. You just need a simple count: **how many tiles of this number have already come out?** With practice, it becomes instinct.

### The Pass: The Most Informative Play in the Game

We underestimate the pass. When someone passes, they show us something invaluable: *they don't have that number*. In pairs, this is pure gold.

If your partner passes when there's a 3 on the board, you know they have no tile with a 3. If they later pass with a 4 open, you've eliminated those two numbers from their hand. You're building an invisible map of what they hold — and what you need to play to help them.

The best pair players communicate through passes as much as through the tiles they place.

### How to Develop Table Memory

Don't expect to remember everything from the first game. Start with a single number. Pick 6, for example, and throughout the entire game track those 7 tiles: how many have come out? When did someone pass when the 6 was showing?

When that becomes automatic, add another number. In a few months, you'll be tracking the whole board without conscious effort.

Repetition builds the pattern. The pattern becomes intuition. Intuition becomes mastery.

### Counting Tiles in Pairs: The Next Level

In individual play, counting tiles gives you an advantage. In pairs, it gives you synchrony.

When you know which numbers your partner has (and doesn't have), you can open the suits that help them and close the ones that hurt them. You don't need signals, looks, or codes. The board speaks — if you know how to listen.

That's what my grandfather was doing that afternoon. He wasn't looking at me because he didn't need to. He had the table read.

### One Last Tip

The next time you have to pass, don't do it silently. Mentally, tell your partner what you just revealed. That's the game within the game. And it's the most beautiful one of all.
